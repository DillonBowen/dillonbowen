About

He doesn't have a tidy origin story.
He has a real one.

The career of Dillon Bowen is less a straight line and more a collection of lessons learned in high-pressure environments. From the red rock job sites of Moab to the logistics of independent film and modern digital marketing, the common thread is a focus on utility — showing up and doing whatever the situation requires.

In hospitality, he moved from the floor to management, learning how to maintain high-volume service without losing composure. In construction, he worked as a form setter and laborer for Straight Line Contracting, handling the technical demands of building foundations on the edge of bedrock — work that required precision, physical endurance, and a focus on safety.

In 2012, Disney's The Lone Ranger arrived in Moab, and Dillon joined the grip team under veteran J. Michael Popovich. On a $250 million production, there is no room for error. He learned that whether you're pouring concrete or lighting a film set, the goal is the same: solve the immediate problem efficiently so the larger project can move forward.

He applied those logistical lessons to the independent film Sorrow. As co-producer, he scouted locations across three states, managed crew logistics in remote areas, and designed a grassroots merchandise campaign that provided the necessary funding to finish the project.

Today, he runs Dilly's Trading Co. — a Minneapolis-based consultancy that applies this generalist mindset to marketing and operations. He works primarily with independent hospitality operators, e-commerce founders, and mission-driven organizations.

Community

The toga party

August 29, 2015. Club Rio, Moab. Two local mothers — Jessica Johnson and Ruth Velasquez — were battling cancer. He pitched the idea, went door-to-door on Main Street soliciting donations from every business in town, secured thousands of dollars in silent auction items, convinced the venue to cover decor and space, and flyered the whole town by bike.

The bar hit capacity. Everyone wore a DIY toga. They raised over $2,000. Covered in the Moab Times-Independent.
